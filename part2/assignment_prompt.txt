MongoDB Querying

(15 marks)

For each query, include the complete pipeline and a concise interpretation of the results. You are
encouraged to visualize the query results wherever possible for easier interpretation. Remember,

1. Perform a cohort analysis of user reviewing behaviour. Define cohorts by the year users joined
Yelp (yelping since). For each annual cohort, compute: the mean and standard deviation
of star ratings given, the mean review character length, the mean useful votes received per
review, and the proportion of reviews in each star category (1–5). Identify which cohort gives
the highest mean star rating and which produces the highest mean useful votes per review.
(5 marks)
2. For each business category with at least 500 reviews, compute the month-over-month change
in average star rating across the full temporal range of the dataset (or whatever range of data
you can load in your system). Measure trend consistency as the proportion of consecutive
month-pairs showing an increase (or decrease) in average rating. Report the three categories
with the most consistent upward trend and the three with the most consistent downward
trend.
(5 marks)
3. Using the check-in data, classify businesses into three groups by total check-in count: top
quartile (high), middle two quartiles (medium), and bottom quartile (low). For each check-in
frequency class and for each of the top 10 business categories by total review count (across
the dataset), compute the mean star rating, mean review count, and ratio of tips to reviews.
Present the results as a cross-tabulation.

Using Cypher and the Neo4j Graph Data Science (GDS) library, answer the following. For each
query, include the complete Cypher statement(s), the GDS procedure calls where applicable, and
a concise interpretation of the results. Visualize the results wherever applicable.
1. Project a graph of User and Business nodes connected by a directed edge from user to
business for each review, weighted by the review’s star rating. Run the PageRank algorithm
(GDS) on this projection with a minimum of 20 iterations. Report the top 15 businesses
by PageRank score alongside their total review count and average star rating. Compute the
Spearman rank correlation between (a) the PageRank ranking and the review-count ranking,
and (b) the PageRank ranking and the average-star-rating ranking. Identify and explain
businesses that rank substantially higher or lower under PageRank than under the simpler
metrics.
(7 marks)
2. Run the Louvain community detection algorithm (GDS) on the FRIENDS WITH social graph.
For each detected community with at least 25 members, report: (1) the community size; (2)
the top three states by number of business reviews written by members of that community; (3)
the top three business categories reviewed by members; (4) and the geographic concentration
index, defined as the fraction of all reviews by community members directed at businesses in
the single most common state. Rank communities by geographic concentration and identify
the most and least geographically concentrated.
(7 marks)
3. For each city with at least 20 businesses, use the Node Similarity algorithm (GDS, Jaccard)
on Business nodes, where two businesses are considered to share an audience if they have
at least 5 common reviewers (if the number of businesses satisfying this criterion is not high
enough for meaningful analysis, reduce the threshold to 4 or 3). Compute the mean intracategory Jaccard similarity score for each category within each city — this measures how
much businesses in the same category and city share their reviewer base, serving as a proxy
for market saturation. A high score indicates that the same pool of customers is reviewing
all businesses in that category (saturated market); a low score indicates each business has a
largely distinct audience (fragmented market). Report the following:
(a) The five city–category combinations with the highest mean intra-category similarity
(most saturated markets) and the five with the lowest (most fragmented), excluding
combinations with fewer than 5 businesses.
(b) For the most and least saturated city–category combinations identified above, compare
the mean star rating, standard deviation of star ratings, and mean review count per business. Discuss whether saturated markets show higher rating variance than fragmented
ones, and what this might imply for a business entering that market.
(7 marks)

Predictive Modelling

(15 marks)

Develop a regression model to predict the number of useful votes a review will receive. Data must
be extracted from the MongoDB and/or Neo4j databases you have already built. You may use any
language or framework including the Neo4j GDS library. Visualize the results & findings through
figures, plots, tables wherever necessary.
1. Feature engineering. Extract a feature set for each review. Your feature set must include
at least one feature from each of the following three groups:
(5 marks)
(a) Review-level features — e.g. star rating, review character length, word count, or recency
of the review.
(b) User-level features — e.g. the reviewing user’s tenure, total review count, elite status,
or mean star rating given.
(c) Graph-derived features — at least two features computed from the graph, such as the
reviewing user’s degree or PageRank score (from Section 6.1), community membership
(from Section 6.2), or the business’s mean cross-category Jaccard similarity (from Section
6.3). Justify why each graph feature is expected to be predictive of useful vote count.
2. Model training and evaluation. Train and evaluate your model using a train/test split,
stratified by useful vote count bucket (0, 1–5, 6–20, 21+) to ensure rare high-vote reviews
appear in both splits. Report RMSE, MAE, and R2 on the test set. Because the useful vote
distribution is heavily right-skewed, also report these metrics separately for reviews with zero
useful votes, 1–5 useful votes, and 6 or more useful votes. Justify your modelling, training
objectives, and evaluation choices.
(5 marks)
3. Feature importance and discussion. Report feature importances or equivalent (e.g.
coefficients, permutation importance, or SHAP values). Identify the three most predictive
features and provide a data-supported explanation for each. Discuss one limitation of the
model and one potential consequence if such a model were used to rank or surface reviews on
the platform.
(5 marks)
